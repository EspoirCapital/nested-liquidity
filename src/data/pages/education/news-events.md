      <h1>News events.</h1>
      <p class="lede">Why news moves markets, how different markets affect each other, and the one rule you should never break: avoid red folder news at all costs.</p>

      <h2>What is a news event?</h2>
      <p>A news event is an economic data release or a central bank announcement. Governments and institutions publish this data on a scheduled calendar — traders know when each release happens. The actual number that comes out is often different from what the market expected, and that difference (the "surprise") is what causes price to move sharply.</p>
      <p>News events are categorized by importance:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Red folder (high impact).</strong> Biggest moves, widest spreads, highest slippage. The market can gap 20–50 pips in under a second.</li>
        <li><strong>Orange folder (medium impact).</strong> Moderate moves. Some volatility but usually manageable.</li>
        <li><strong>Yellow folder (low impact).</strong> Minimal effect on price. Usually safe to ignore.</li>
      </ul>
      <p><strong style="color:var(--accent);">The rule: never trade through a red folder news event. Ever.</strong> More on this at the bottom of the page.</p>

      <h2>How different markets affect each other</h2>
      <p>Markets don't move in isolation. A news event in one market ripples through others. Understanding these connections helps you predict what might happen even if you don't trade that specific market.</p>

      <p><strong>Dollar strength affects everything.</strong> The USD is on one side of 90% of all forex trades. When the dollar strengthens, USD-denominated assets become more expensive for foreign buyers. This means:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>USD up → Commodities (gold, oil, silver) down (they're priced in USD)</li>
        <li>USD up → EUR/USD, GBP/USD, AUD/USD down (you're selling these pairs to buy dollars)</li>
        <li>USD up → USD/JPY, USD/CHF up (you're buying these pairs with dollars)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Commodities feed currency pairs.</strong> Countries that export natural resources see their currencies move with commodity prices:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Oil up → CAD up (Canada is a major oil exporter). USD/CAD goes down.</li>
        <li>Oil up → NOK up (Norway). Similar logic.</li>
        <li>Gold up → AUD up (Australia is a major gold exporter). AUD/USD goes up.</li>
        <li>Gold up → NZD up (New Zealand). NZD/USD goes up.</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Risk sentiment (risk-on vs risk-off).</strong> When traders feel confident, they buy "risk-on" assets. When they're scared, they flee to "safe havens":</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Risk-on</strong> → Stocks up, crypto up, commodity currencies (AUD, NZD, CAD) up, USD/JPY up (buying JPY with dollars = risk)</li>
        <li><strong>Risk-off</strong> → Gold up (safe haven), USD up (safe haven), JPY up (safe haven), CHF up (safe haven). Stock markets down, crypto down, AUD/NZD/CAD down</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Major red folder news events — explained simply</h2>

      <h3>Interest Rate Decision (Central Bank)</h3>
      <p><em>Release: 8 times per year per central bank (Fed, ECB, BOE, BOJ, RBA, RBNZ, SNB).</em></p>
      <p>The central bank sets the interest rate — the cost of borrowing money. If rates go up, the currency becomes more attractive to hold (you earn more interest). If rates stay the same or go down, the currency weakens. The market cares less about the actual rate and more about the <strong>forward guidance</strong> — what the bank says about future rate changes. The minutes released 2–3 weeks later can move the market just as much.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> 30–60 minutes of volatility. Price can whip back and forth before choosing a direction. Spreads widen to 5–10x normal. Do not hold a position through this release.</p>

      <h3>Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP)</h3>
      <p><em>Release: First Friday of every month at 1:30 PM GMT (8:30 AM EST).</em></p>
      <p>The US government reports how many jobs were added outside farming. It's widely considered a key economic indicator for the USD. A strong number suggests the economy is growing (USD up), a weak number suggests the economy is struggling (USD down). But the <strong>previous month's data is also revised</strong>, so the headline number isn't the only thing that matters — revisions can be bigger than the surprise itself.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> 30–90 seconds of violent initial spike, then a reversal as the market digests the data. Spreads blow out to 20+ pips during the first few seconds. Many traders prefer to wait until after the initial spike settles before looking for entries.</p>

      <h3>CPI (Consumer Price Index) — Inflation</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Monthly, usually mid-month.</em></p>
      <p>Measures how much prices have risen for everyday goods and services. The number everyone watches is "CPI YoY" (compared to the same month last year). Higher than expected means inflation is hot → central bank likely to raise rates → currency strengthens. Lower than expected means inflation is cooling → currency weakens. There's also <strong>Core CPI</strong> (excludes food and energy) which the Fed pays more attention to than the headline number.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Similar volatility to NFP. In recent years, CPI is widely watched as a key market-moving release because inflation directly influences interest rate decisions.</p>

      <h3>GDP (Gross Domestic Product)</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Quarterly (advance, preliminary, final).</em></p>
      <p>Measures the total value of everything a country produces. GDP growth = economy growing = good for currency. GDP shrinking = recession = bad for currency. The "advance" release is the first estimate and moves the market most. Subsequent revisions usually have less impact.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Moderate to high volatility depending on how much it deviates from forecast. Often moves the market over hours rather than minutes.</p>

      <h3>FOMC Statement & Press Conference</h3>
      <p><em>Release: 8 times per year, 2:00 PM EST (statement) followed by press conference 30 minutes later.</em></p>
      <p>The Fed's policy statement explains what they decided on rates and why. The press conference with the Fed chair is where the real action happens — his tone (hawkish = pro-rate hikes, dovish = pro-rate cuts) can reverse the initial reaction within minutes. Traders parse <em>every word</em> for changes in phrasing like "patient" vs "vigilant" or "transitory" vs "persistent."</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Two waves — immediate reaction to the statement at 2:00 PM, then a reversal or acceleration during the press conference at 2:30 PM. This is one of the most dangerous events to hold through because the press conference can change everything.</p>

      <h3>Retail Sales</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Monthly, usually mid-month.</em></p>
      <p>Measures how much money consumers spent at stores. Consumer spending is the engine of the US economy (70% of GDP). Strong retail sales → people have money and confidence → economy growing → USD up. This is a "consumption" indicator — the market uses it as a proxy for the health of the average person.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Moderate to high volatility. Often gets overlooked compared to NFP/CPI but can be just as sharp.</p>

      <h3>Unemployment Rate / Jobless Claims</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Unemployment rate monthly (same day as NFP). Jobless claims every Thursday.</em></p>
      <p>The unemployment rate measures the percentage of people looking for work who can't find it. Low unemployment = tight labor market = pressure to raise wages = potential inflation = possible rate hikes. Weekly jobless claims measure how many people filed for unemployment benefits in the past week — it's a more frequent, less impactful version of the same data.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> The monthly unemployment rate moves markets (it's released alongside NFP). Weekly claims usually don't move price much unless the number is completely unexpected.</p>

      <h3>PPI (Producer Price Index)</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Monthly, a few days after CPI.</em></p>
      <p>Like CPI but for what businesses pay for goods. PPI is a "leading indicator" — if producers are paying more, those costs eventually pass to consumers (showing up in CPI months later). The market watches PPI for early signs of inflation pressure.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> High volatility, slightly less than CPI but still red folder. Especially important when the market is focused on inflation (which it is in 2024–2026).</p>

      <h3>ISM Manufacturing / Services PMI</h3>
      <p><em>Release: First business day of the month (Manufacturing), third business day (Services).</em></p>
      <p>Surveys of purchasing managers in manufacturing and services sectors. A reading above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction. The Services PMI carries more weight because services make up a larger share of the US economy.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Moderate to high volatility. Can trend-drive price for the rest of the day.</p>

      <h3>Housing Data (Housing Starts, Building Permits, Existing Home Sales)</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Monthly, mid-month.</em></p>
      <p>Measures the health of the housing market. Housing is interest-rate sensitive — when rates are high, fewer people buy houses. Strong housing data means the economy is resilient. Weak housing data means high rates are biting.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Moderate volatility. Usually doesn't cause the violent spikes that NFP/CPI do, but can set the tone for the session.</p>

      <h3>Consumer Confidence / Michigan Sentiment</h3>
      <p><em>Release: Monthly. Consumer Confidence (Conference Board) mid-month. Michigan Sentiment monthly (preliminary and final).</em></p>
      <p>Surveys asking consumers how they feel about the economy. If people feel good about their jobs and income, they spend more, which drives economic growth. Forward-looking, but less reliable than hard data like CPI or retail sales.</p>
      <p><strong>Expect:</strong> Low to moderate volatility. Moves the market occasionally when the number significantly misses expectations.</p>

      <h2>The red folder rule — never break this.</h2>
      <p>This is the most important rule in this strategy. It's more important than any entry or exit rule.</p>
      <p><strong style="color:var(--accent); font-size:18px;">Do not hold a trade through a red folder news event. Do not enter a trade 15 minutes before a red folder release. Do not enter a trade 5 minutes after a red folder release.</strong></p>
      <p>Here's why:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Slippage destroys you.</strong> Your stop loss can fill 20–50 pips below where you set it. A 20-pip stop becomes a 60-pip loss instantly. The strategy's risk management assumes clean fills — news events break that assumption.</li>
        <li><strong>Spreads widen 10–100x.</strong> EUR/USD normally has a 0.5–1 pip spread. During NFP it can spike to 20+ pips. Your trade starts in negative territory that you may never recover from.</li>
        <li><strong>False breaks are the norm.</strong> The initial spike often reverses violently. The exact same news can push price up 30 pips, then down 50 pips within 60 seconds. The NH/NL levels you identified mean nothing during this chaos.</li>
        <li><strong>Your strategy doesn't work during news.</strong> The Nested Liquidity strategy is built on institutional order flow and price action patterns. News events override all of that — the market moves based on data, not on level breaks. FVGs, sweeps, and NH/NL levels are unreliable during and immediately after major releases.</li>
        <li><strong>Gaps.</strong> Price can open far below your stop loss or take profit, meaning you miss your target or blow past your stop without any chance of exit.</li>
      </ul>
      <p><strong>What to do instead:</strong> Check the economic calendar before every trading session. <a href="https://www.forexfactory.com/calendar" target="_blank" rel="noopener">ForexFactory Calendar</a> and <a href="https://financialjuice.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">FinancialJuice</a> are two widely used free sources — ForexFactory for the full schedule with red/orange/yellow folders, FinancialJuice for live news headlines as they break. Know which red folder events are coming. Close all positions 30 minutes before the release. Wait at least 15–30 minutes after the release for the market to settle. Then and only then look for NH/NL setups on the new structure that emerged after the news.</p>
      <p>Think of it this way: if you trade through a red folder event, you aren't trading the strategy. You're gambling on a binary data release. And gambling is not a strategy.</p>
