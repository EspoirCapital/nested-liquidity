      <h1>Trading markets.</h1>
      <p class="lede">CFDs, futures, options — what each market is, and why this strategy uses CFDs.</p>

      <h2>CFDs (Contract for Difference)</h2>
      <p>A CFD is an agreement between you and the broker to exchange the difference in price of an asset from when you open the trade to when you close it. You never own the underlying asset — you're speculating on price movement.</p>
      <p><strong>This strategy trades CFDs.</strong> Here's why CFDs are the right vehicle:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Leverage.</strong> You control a large position with a small deposit. Typical leverage is 1:30 to 1:500 depending on regulation. This lets you trade $100k notional with a few thousand dollars in margin.</li>
        <li><strong>No expiry.</strong> Unlike futures, CFD positions don't have an expiration date. You can hold a trade as long as the trend requires — relevant for swing trades on NH/NL levels.</li>
        <li><strong>Easy short selling.</strong> Going short is as simple as clicking "sell." No uptick rules, no borrowing shares. This is essential for a strategy that trades both long (NL sweep → buy) and short (NH sweep → sell).</li>
        <li><strong>No ownership costs.</strong> No stamp duty, no custody fees. The only cost is the spread and potentially swap if held overnight.</li>
      </ul>
      <p><strong>The downside:</strong> You don't own the asset. You're trading a derivative with the broker as counterparty. Losses can exceed deposits if you overleverage (always use stops). Some brokers also widen spreads or restrict trading during volatile events.</p>

      <h2>Futures</h2>
      <p>A futures contract is a binding agreement to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date. Traded on exchanges (CME, NYMEX, etc.) with central clearing.</p>
      <p><strong>Key differences from CFDs:</strong> Fixed contract sizes, expiration dates (must roll over or settle), exchange-traded (transparent pricing), no counterparty risk (clearing house guarantees). Futures use margin too, but the calculations are different.</p>
      <p><strong>Why not use futures for this strategy?</strong> The NH/NL setup works on price action across multiple timeframes. Futures have fixed lot sizes (e.g., 1 E-mini S&P = $50 per point), which makes precise position sizing harder for small accounts. CFDs give you micro lots and more flexibility. If you have larger capital, futures are a viable alternative with better transparency.</p>

      <h2>Options</h2>
      <p>Options give you the <em>right</em> (not obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a specific price before a specific date. You pay a premium for this right. Options are used for hedging, income (selling premium), or leveraged directional bets with defined risk.</p>
      <p><strong>Why not use options for this strategy?</strong> Options have time decay (theta). The Nested Liquidity setup can take hours or days to develop — time decay eats into the position. The sweep-and-confirm model is also too dynamic for predefined strike prices. Options are better for slower, trend-based strategies.</p>

      <h2>Stocks / ETFs</h2>
      <p>Buying shares means owning a piece of a company. Leverage is limited (typically 1:2 to 1:5 in margin accounts). Short selling requires borrowing shares and paying interest.</p>
      <p><strong>Not suitable for this strategy.</strong> The leverage is too low, short selling is restrictive, and the price action patterns (FVGs, sweeps) are less reliable on individual stocks compared to forex indices where liquidity is deeper.</p>

      <h2>Cryptocurrency</h2>
      <p>Crypto is traded on both centralized exchanges (Binance, Bybit, Coinbase) and as CFDs through forex brokers. The difference matters: <strong>exchange trading</strong> uses spot or perpetual futures contracts with the exchange as counterparty, while <strong>crypto CFDs</strong> with a forex broker use the same CFD model as forex pairs.</p>
      <p><strong>Crypto CFDs (recommended for this strategy).</strong> Traded through your regular forex broker alongside forex pairs. Same benefits: leverage, easy short selling, no wallet or exchange account needed. BTC/USD and ETH/USD are the most liquid pairs. Spreads are wider than forex but reasonable during active hours.</p>
      <p><strong>Crypto exchanges (spot / perpetuals).</strong> Binance and Bybit offer high leverage (up to 1:100) on perpetual futures. However, the environment is different: 24/7 trading (no sessions), extreme volatility, and different market structure (crypto tends to have larger gaps and less clean FVGs). The NH/NL strategy can work on crypto if you use higher timeframes (1h–4h) but expect more noise.</p>
      <p><strong>Watch out for:</strong> Crypto CFDs through forex brokers often have higher spreads, no weekend trading (most forex brokers close crypto on weekends), and lower leverage than crypto-native exchanges. If crypto is your primary market, a dedicated exchange like Bybit may be better. If you occasionally trade crypto alongside forex, crypto CFDs through your broker are more convenient.</p>

      <h2>Forex Spot</h2>
      <p>Trading currency pairs directly (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, etc.) without a CFD wrapper. Very similar to forex CFDs in practice — leveraged, no expiry, 24-hour market. The main difference is how the trade settles (T+2 in spot forex) and regulatory classification. Most retail "forex" trading is actually CFD trading in everything but name.</p>

      <h2>The important distinction</h2>
      <p>Whether you trade CFDs, futures, or spot forex, the <strong>strategy logic is the same</strong> — you're looking for NH/NL levels, waiting for sweeps, and entering on IFVG confirmation. The difference is in execution: CFD brokers offer more flexibility for small accounts, futures offer better transparency for larger accounts. This documentation assumes CFD trading because that's what most retail traders use to access forex and indices with reasonable leverage and position sizing.</p>
