      <h1>Brokers.</h1>
      <p class="lede">What a broker actually does, how they make money, and what to look for — explained for traders who just want to understand the game.</p>

      <h2>What is a broker?</h2>
      <p>A broker is the company that connects you to the market. You can't trade forex directly — there's no "forex exchange" like the stock market. Instead, brokers act as the middleman: you place a buy or sell order through their platform, and they find a way to fill it. Without a broker, you can't trade.</p>

      <h2>How brokers make money</h2>
      <p>Brokers make money in two ways: <strong>spreads</strong> and <strong>commissions</strong>. The spread is the difference between the buy price and the sell price. If EUR/USD has a spread of 1 pip, you start every trade 1 pip underwater — that's the broker's cut. Some brokers charge commission instead (a flat fee per lot) and give you near-zero spreads.</p>
      <p>But there's a second, less obvious way brokers make money, and it depends on how they handle your order. This is where the model matters.</p>

      <h2>How your order gets handled</h2>
      <p>When you click "buy," the broker has two choices:</p>
      <p><strong>Option 1 — Pass it to the market.</strong> The broker sends your order to a bigger player (a bank or liquidity provider) who actually has the asset. The broker charges you a small fee for the connection. This is called A-Book. The broker doesn't care if you win or lose — they just collect their fee on every trade.</p>
      <p><strong>Option 2 — Keep it in-house.</strong> The broker takes the other side of your trade. You buy, they sell. If the trade goes in your favor, the broker loses money. If it goes against you, the broker keeps what you lost. This is called B-Book. The broker is essentially betting against you.</p>
      <p>Most brokers do <strong>both</strong>. They B-Book traders who lose consistently (profiting from their losses) and A-Book traders who win consistently (passing the risk to the market so they don't lose money when you win). The broker's system decides which bucket you fall into based on your trading history.</p>

      <h2>Execution models explained</h2>
      <p><strong>ECN (Electronic Communication Network).</strong> Your order goes into a network of banks and institutions. You see the real market prices with no broker interference. Tight spreads, pay a commission per trade. Best for serious traders who want transparency.</p>
      <p><strong>STP (Straight Through Processing).</strong> Your order goes directly through the broker to a liquidity provider without anyone touching it. Similar to ECN but slightly less direct — the broker may add a tiny markup to the spread instead of charging commission.</p>
      <p><strong>Market Maker (Dealing Desk).</strong> The broker is the counterparty to every trade. They set their own prices (usually slightly wider spreads) and you trade against them. Newer traders often start here because minimum deposits are low and prices are stable — but the conflict of interest is real.</p>

      <h2>Costs you need to understand</h2>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Spread.</strong> The gap between bid and ask. Measured in pips. On EUR/USD, a spread of 1 pip means you pay $10 per standard lot round trip. This is your main cost.</li>
        <li><strong>Commission.</strong> A flat fee per trade. ECN brokers charge $3–$7 per standard lot round turn. Combined with tight spreads, this is often cheaper than wide spreads with no commission.</li>
        <li><strong>Swap / Overnight Fee.</strong> Interest charged or earned for holding a position overnight. For day trading IFVG setups, this matters less — but if you hold swings, check swap rates.</li>
        <li><strong>Slippage.</strong> The difference between the price you expected and the price you got. More common during news events and with B-Book brokers when you're winning.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Regulation</h2>
      <p>Not all brokers are created equal. A regulated broker has to follow rules, keep client funds in separate accounts, and submit to audits. An unregulated broker can disappear with your money and you have no recourse.</p>
      <p><strong>Strong regulation:</strong> FCA (UK), ASIC (Australia), DFSA (Dubai), BaFin (Germany). These are gold standard — strict capital requirements, negative balance protection.</p>
      <p><strong>Medium regulation:</strong> CySEC (Cyprus), FSA (Seychelles). Some oversight but weaker enforcement. Many retail brokers operate under CySEC.</p>
      <p><strong>Weak / No regulation:</strong> Offshore brokers (SVG, Vanuatu). Cheap to set up, almost no oversight. Higher risk.</p>
      <p>If a broker offers "bonuses" or "prizes," that's usually a red flag — heavily regulated brokers don't do this.</p>

      <h2>What to look for in a broker for this strategy</h2>
      <p>The Nested Liquidity strategy needs fast and reliable execution. When you spot an IFVG on the LTF, you need to enter immediately — a slow broker will cost you money.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>ECN or STP execution</strong> — avoid Market Makers if you're consistently profitable. They have no incentive to give you a fair fill.</li>
        <li><strong>Floating spreads</strong> — you want tight spreads during the sessions you trade (London, New York). Fixed spreads are usually wider.</li>
        <li><strong>Fast withdrawal</strong> — if you're winning, you want your money. Check reviews for withdrawal speed.</li>
        <li><strong>MetaTrader 5 or MatchTrader</strong> — these are the platforms most prop firms use, so using them with your broker too keeps things consistent.</li>
        <li><strong>Decent leverage</strong> — 1:30 to 1:500 depending on regulation. Enough to trade meaningfully without overleveraging.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>If you're starting small, go with a regulated ECN/STP broker. The few dollars more in commission is worth the peace of mind that your trades are being handled fairly.</p>
