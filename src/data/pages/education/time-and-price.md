      <h1>Time and price.</h1>
      <p class="lede">Every candlestick is a negotiation between time (the x-axis) and price (the y-axis). Reading that negotiation helps you see where buyers and sellers are winning or losing the battle.</p>

      <h2>What is a candlestick?</h2>
      <p>A candlestick is a unit of time showing four prices: <strong>open</strong> (where price started), <strong>high</strong> (where it pushed to), <strong>low</strong> (where it rejected to), and <strong>close</strong> (where it ended up). Everything that happened between open and close is compressed into those four numbers.</p>
      <p>On a <strong>1-minute chart</strong>, each candle is 60 seconds of trading. On a <strong>15-minute chart</strong>, each candle is 900 seconds. Same four data points, different time scale. The time unit is the lens — it determines how much detail you see.</p>

      <h2>The body tells you who won</h2>
      <p>The <strong>body</strong> of the candle is the distance between open and close. It's the result of the time period:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Bullish candle (close above open):</strong> Buyers had the upper hand. Price started at one level and ended higher. Green/white body.</li>
        <li><strong>Bearish candle (close below open):</strong> Sellers had the upper hand. Price started higher and ended lower. Red/black body.</li>
        <li><strong>Long body:</strong> One side dominated. Strong momentum in that direction.</li>
        <li><strong>Small body / doji (open = close):</strong> Neither side won. Indecision. The battle was a draw.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>A single candle's body tells you who won that specific time unit. A series of candles with growing bullish bodies tells you buyers are in control. A series with growing bearish bodies tells you sellers are in control. Mixed bodies means the market is deciding.</p>

      <h2>The wick tells you where the battle was lost</h2>
      <p>The <strong>wick</strong> (also called shadow or tail) is the thin line above and below the body. It shows where price visited but couldn't stay. Wicks are rejection — the market tried to go further but was pushed back.</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Upper wick:</strong> Buyers tried to push higher but sellers rejected them at that level. Price was "too expensive" and got sold.</li>
        <li><strong>Lower wick:</strong> Sellers tried to push lower but buyers rejected them at that level. Price was "too cheap" and got bought.</li>
        <li><strong>Long upper wick on a bullish candle:</strong> Buyers won the period, but they're losing control at the highs. Warning sign.</li>
        <li><strong>Long lower wick on a bearish candle:</strong> Sellers won the period, but they're losing control at the lows. Potential reversal signal.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>In the NH/NL strategy, wicks are critical. A sweep happens when price <em>wicks</em> through a level (touches it briefly) but doesn't close through it. That wick is the footprint of the sweep — the market reaching for liquidity and then rejecting it.</p>

      <h2>Timeframe is your lens</h2>
      <p>The same price action looks completely different on different timeframes. Choosing the right timeframe is like choosing the right lens — zoom in too far and you miss the structure, zoom out too far and you miss the detail.</p>

      <h2>Price + time = context</h2>
      <p>A candle's shape alone doesn't tell you everything. The <em>time</em> at which it formed adds context — who was trading, what markets were open, what influenced the move. The same candle shape at different times of day happened under different conditions:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Candle at 2:00 AM UTC:</strong> Forms during the Asian session. Tokyo financial markets are open.</li>
        <li><strong>Candle at 8:00 AM UTC (winter):</strong> Forms during the London open. European markets are now active alongside Asia's tail end.</li>
        <li><strong>Candle at 13:00 PM UTC (winter):</strong> Forms during the London/NY overlap. European and US markets are both open.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Each window has different participants, different instruments driving price, and different levels of activity. The same price move at different times may have different significance depending on who was behind it — but that's something to observe in your own trading, not assume. Check the Trading Sessions page for what drives each session for your specific pair.</p>

      <h2>How this connects to the NH/NL strategy</h2>
      <p>Every NH/NL setup is built on candles. Here's exactly how candle reading flows into the strategy:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Identifying the level (HTF).</strong> An NH is a swing high formed by a sequence of candles making higher highs. You spot it by looking at M15 candles. An NL is the opposite — candles making lower lows. The FVG happens when three consecutive candles create a gap between candle 1's low and candle 3's high (bullish FVG) or candle 1's high and candle 3's low (bearish FVG).</li>
        <li><strong>Waiting for the sweep.</strong> Price returns to the level and <em>wicks</em> through it — the wick is the sweep. Price touched the level but didn't close beyond it. The wick is literally the footprint of the market reaching for liquidity.</li>
        <li><strong>LTF entries.</strong> The IFVG entry is a candle-based signal on M2/M3. You're watching for the same three-candle FVG pattern but on a lower timeframe, at the exact moment price sweeps the HTF level.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>In short: <strong>the HTF candle structure tells you WHERE (the level). The LTF candle structure tells you WHEN (the entry).</strong> Every decision in this strategy comes back to reading candles on two timeframes — time and price, working together.</p>

      <h2>One more thing — timeframes are relative</h2>
      <p>There's nothing special about M15 vs M2 specifically. What matters is the <em>ratio</em> between your HTF and LTF. The HTF should be 3-5x larger than the LTF so the structure is clear but you're not waiting too long for confirmation. M15/M2, M15/M3, M30/M5, H1/M15 — all work as long as the higher timeframe gives you clean structure and the lower timeframe gives you timely entries within that structure.</p>
