      <h1>Philosophy</h1>
      <p class="lede">The foundation of this strategy is accepting what the market actually is, not what we wish it were.</p>
      <p>We cannot predict the future. Anything can happen at any moment — a sudden news event, an unexpected institutional order, a flash crash. The market is inherently unpredictable, and no amount of analysis changes that.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"The goal of a successful trader is to make the best trades. Money is secondary."</p>
        <cite>Alexander Elder</cite>
      </div>
      <p>This is why traders are not analysts. An analyst tries to forecast where the market will go. A trader does not need to. You do not need to know the direction. You do not need to know if a move is a retracement or a full reversal. You do not know when the retracement will end — and you never will.</p>
      <p>What you do know is this: the longer you stay in a trade, the more time you are exposed to risk. Every additional minute in a position increases the probability of being caught by something unpredictable. Time in the trade is exposure, and exposure is risk.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"You do not need to predict the future to be profitable. You need a method, discipline, and the willingness to accept uncertainty."</p>
        <cite>Tom Hougaard, Best Loser Wins</cite>
      </div>
      <p>This is why this strategy targets a small piece of the move — a slice of the retracement as price reverses from a swept level. You do not need to catch the whole move. You do not need to ride it to its end. You take what the reversal gives you, quickly, and you get out.</p>
      <p>This discipline extends to how often you trade. One session. One trade. That is not a restriction — it is a recognition that the best setups are rare, and chasing volume destroys the edge that patience creates. When you take one clean entry per session, you remove the urge to force trades out of boredom or frustration. You wait for the level to present itself, you execute, and you walk away. The market will be there tomorrow. Your account will be there tomorrow. That is the point.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"The market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient."</p>
        <cite>Warren Buffett</cite>
      </div>
      <p>This approach is rooted in alignment, not force. In Taoist philosophy there is a concept called <em>Wu Wei</em> — effortless action, or doing by not doing. It does not mean passivity. It means acting only when the conditions are right, without struggle, without forcing an outcome. The river does not push the rock out of its way. It flows around it.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"Nothing in the world is as soft and yielding as water. Yet for dissolving the hard and inflexible, nothing can surpass it."</p>
        <cite>Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching</cite>
      </div>
      <p>Trading is the same. You do not force entries. You wait for alignment — when the level is swept, when the lower timeframe confirms, when the setup presents itself cleanly. When all pieces align, the trade feels almost easy. That is the flow state: not predicting, not chasing, but responding to what is already happening in front of you.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"Your only goal in trading is to trade well. Not to make money. The money is a byproduct of trading well."</p>
        <cite>Mark Douglas, Trading in the Zone</cite>
      </div>
      <p>Low risk-to-reward and high win rate is not a compromise. It is the only honest approach when you accept that the market is random, the future is unknown, and time is the enemy. Zen is not about calm — it is about presence. Seeing the market as it is, without the noise of what you want it to be. The moment you stop forcing, you start trading.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"The hard part is not knowing what to do. The hard part is doing it when you don't feel like it."</p>
        <cite>Mark Douglas, Trading in the Zone</cite>
      </div>
      <p>The most important job as a trader is not to make money — it is to protect what you already have. Every decision, every entry, every exit is a question of risk first and profit second. That mindset is the difference between surviving and blowing up.</p>
      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"The best traders are not the ones who make the most money when they are right. They are the ones who lose the least when they are wrong."</p>
        <cite>Tom Hougaard, Best Loser Wins</cite>
      </div>
