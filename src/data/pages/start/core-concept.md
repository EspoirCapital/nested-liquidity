      <h1>Core Concept</h1>
      <p class="lede">Nested Liquidity describes a swing high or swing low that sits inside a Fair Value Gap.</p>
      <p>To understand why this matters, you need to understand what liquidity and an FVG are.</p>
      <div class="def-box">
        <div class="db-label">Liquidity</div>
        <p>Liquidity is where stop losses rest. At every swing high and swing low, traders have placed stops — and market makers need that liquidity to fill large orders. Price is drawn to these levels like a magnet.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="def-box">
        <div class="db-label">Fair Value Gap (FVG)</div>
        <p>An FVG is a three-candle imbalance — a gap where price moved so quickly that it left an unfilled zone. These imbalances tend to get "filled" as price returns to balance them out.</p>
      </div>
      <p>When a swing high or swing low sits inside an FVG, that's Nested Liquidity. The FVG acts as a shield — price hasn't cleanly closed through that zone yet, so the liquidity at that swing point is still there, untouched.</p>
      <div class="callout warn">
        <strong>The Edge —</strong> There are two things pulling price to a nested level. First, the FVG — price moved too fast and left an imbalance. Price naturally returns to fill that gap. Second, the liquidity — stop losses are sitting at the swing point inside the FVG. When price reaches that level, it sweeps those stops, and institutions use that liquidity to fill their own large orders. Once they're done, the buying or selling pressure is finished — price reverses. You're trading that reversal.
      </div>
